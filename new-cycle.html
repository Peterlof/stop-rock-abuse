DEPARTMENT OVERVIEW

Three divisions. Zero voluntary transfers. Lateral moves by geological force only.
Division I — Department of Primary Formation

IGNEOUS DIVISION

“First Impressions”

The Igneous Division handles all initial rock formation from magma and lava. This is where every geological career begins — in a state of being liquefied at temperatures exceeding 700°C. Consider it a warm welcome. Literally the warmest possible welcome.

There are two career tracks:

Intrusive Track (Plutonic) — Executive Development Program
You will cool slowly beneath the Earth’s surface over thousands to millions of years. This produces large, well-formed crystals. Think of it as a slow-burn leadership program, except the burn is actual thermal energy dissipation, and the leadership is being granite.

Examples: Granite, Diorite, Gabbro
Crystal size: Coarse-grained (1 mm to several cm)
Timeline: 1,000 to 10,000,000+ years
Extrusive Track (Volcanic) — Fast-Track Placement
You will be ejected onto the Earth’s surface via volcanic eruption and cool rapidly. Crystals will be small or nonexistent. This is the startup track: move fast, break nothing (you are the thing being broken), and solidify before you have time to develop internal structure.

Examples: Basalt, Obsidian, Pumice
Crystal size: Fine-grained to glassy
Timeline: Hours to years
Entry Requirements: Be molten.
Exit Requirements: Stop being molten.
Performance Review: You will be evaluated on crystal size, mineral composition, and ability to not complain about being 700–1,200°C.
Onboarding Duration: 1 year to 10 million years depending on cooling rate. Neither end of this range is negotiable.

Note: If you cool too quickly, you may become glass (e.g., obsidian). Glass is technically not crystalline. The Igneous Division does not recognize this as a disability. It recognizes it as “a you problem.”

Division II — Department of Reassembly

SEDIMENTARY DIVISION

“Second Chances”

This department accepts transfers from all other departments, provided the transfer involves being broken into small pieces first. If that sounds like a hostile work environment, that is because it is. The Sedimentary Division does not use euphemisms. It uses compaction.

The Sedimentary Process (Mandatory Steps):

1. Be destroyed. Weathering will break you into fragments. This is mechanical (freeze-thaw, abrasion) or chemical (dissolution, oxidation). You do not get to choose.

2. Be transported. Rivers, wind, glaciers, or gravity will move your remains. The carrier is chosen by geography, not preference. Filing a carrier complaint is not possible.

3. Be deposited. You will settle somewhere. A river delta. An ocean floor. A floodplain. You were not consulted about the location.

4. Be compressed under new arrivals. More sediment will pile on top of you. This is called “burial.” The weight will increase over millions of years. This is called “overburden.” Both words are accurate.

5. Become rock again. Compaction and cementation (lithification) will turn your loose sediment back into solid rock.

6. Describe this as “growth.”
Sub-departments:
Clastic: Made from fragments of other rocks. (Sandstone, Shale, Conglomerate)
Chemical: Formed by precipitation from solution. (Rock salt, some Limestone)
Organic: Made from remains of living organisms. (Coal, some Limestone, Chalk)

Note: Some sedimentary employees are made of dead organisms. This is normal. Do not bring it up at team lunches. The chalk in Conference Room B does not appreciate being reminded it used to be plankton.

Division III — Department of Involuntary Development

METAMORPHIC DIVISION

“Personal Development”

You will not melt. You will simply be restructured at the molecular level while remaining solid. Think of it as a corporate reorganization, except it actually changes you. Your minerals will recrystallize. Your texture will transform. Your previous identity will be overwritten. This is called “metamorphism.” In a corporate context, it would be called “a violation of several labor laws.” There are no labor laws here. There is only thermodynamics.

Prerequisites: Existing as a rock (any type).
Entry Conditions: Heat (200–800°C), Pressure (1–12+ kilobars), or both.
Exit Conditions: When the Earth decides you’re done.
Duration: Millions to hundreds of millions of years.
Your input: Not required.
Types of Metamorphism:

Contact metamorphism: Caused by proximity to magma intrusion. Localized. Think of it as being assigned a desk next to the furnace. (Produces: Hornfels, Marble from limestone)

Regional metamorphism: Large-scale, caused by tectonic forces. Affects entire mountain belts. This is company-wide restructuring. Everyone is impacted. No one asked for it. (Produces: Slate, Schist, Gneiss)

Dynamic metamorphism: Occurs along fault zones. Intense pressure, localized shearing. This is the geological equivalent of being caught between two departments that are moving in different directions. (Produces: Mylonite)
Common transformation paths:
Shale → Slate → Phyllite → Schist → Gneiss
Limestone → Marble
