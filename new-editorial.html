Paper Beats Rock: A Critical Reexamination of Game-Theoretic Assumptions in Hand-Signal Resolution Systems

Dr. A. Gneiss1, Prof. B. Schist2
1Department of Injustice Studies, University of the Mantle  •  2Center for Recreational Bias Analysis, Cratonic Institute
Received: 4.6 Ga  |  Accepted: Still pending  |  Published: Anyway
Abstract
This paper examines the foundational claim of the hand-signal resolution system known as “Rock, Paper, Scissors” — specifically, the assertion that paper defeats rock. We subject this claim to physical testing across 47 paper-rock interactions and find that in 100% of cases, rock defeats paper by every measurable metric including mass, durability, and damage inflicted. We conclude that the game’s rules represent a systematic anti-rock bias embedded in childhood education worldwide. We call for immediate reform, or at minimum, an asterisk.
Key Passage §3.1
Place a sheet of standard A4 copy paper (80 gsm, 0.1mm thick) over a piece of granite. The rock is now slightly less visible. It has not been defeated. It has not been contained. It has been mildly inconvenienced in terms of direct sunlight exposure. By this logic, a hat defeats the human head, a tablecloth defeats a table, and nightfall defeats everything. The methodology is, to use a technical term, nonsense.
Key Passage §4.2
Children encounter rock-paper-scissors at age 3–4, years before they learn what rocks actually are. Their first formal relationship with geology is a game that tells them rocks lose to office supplies. By the time they take Earth Science in middle school, the damage is done. They have internalized, at a neurodevelopmental level, that paper is superior to stone. We do not argue this is the sole cause of geological illiteracy, but we note the correlation and raise an eyebrow.

The authors further note that “scissors beats paper” is at least physically coherent — scissors can cut paper. “Rock beats scissors” is similarly defensible — a rock will destroy scissors. It is only the paper-rock interaction that relies on metaphor rather than physics. The game is two-thirds empirical and one-third propaganda.

Peer Review Comments
While methodologically sound, the authors fail to consider that “paper” in the original Chinese game (shǒuqūn) may have represented a decree or legal document. The argument that bureaucracy defeats nature is… actually quite compelling. I’m troubled. Accept with revisions.
I showed this to my children. They now refuse to play the game. My eldest insists on resolving disputes by comparing the Mohs hardness of nearby objects. Family dinners have become longer. Reject on ethical grounds.
We recognize this paper may cause distress to paper-first players. A support hotline has not been established because this is a game. We also note that Reviewer 2’s eldest child sounds like an excellent future geologist.

Commodification Under Glass: The Pet Rock as Economic Case Study in Manufactured Dependency (1975–1976)

Dr. C.Ite1, Dr. D. Ite1 (no relation)
1School of Geological Economics, Rosarito Beach Institute
Received: 4.6 Ga  |  Accepted: Reluctantly  |  Published: With reservations (like the Pet Rock)
Abstract
In 1975, Gary Dahl sold approximately 1.5 million rocks at $3.95 each, netting roughly $85 million in today’s dollars. This paper examines the Pet Rock phenomenon as a case study in manufactured dependency — specifically, the inclusion of air holes in the carrying box for an organism that does not breathe. We trace the arc from novelty to cultural artifact and conclude that the Pet Rock was, in economic terms, the most honest product ever sold: it promised nothing, delivered nothing, and the customer knew this going in. Every product since has been less transparent.
Key Passage §2.3
The air holes served no functional purpose. They implied life where none existed, created the suggestion of a need that would justify enclosure. This is the foundational move of commodification — manufacture dependency to legitimize the product. The Pet Rock did not need air. It did not need the box. It did not need you. The air holes were theatre, and the audience paid $3.95 for the performance.
Key Passage §5.1
The crystal healing industry performs identical extraction with a different markup. A piece of rose quartz that spent 50 million years forming in a pegmatite body retails for $24 on Etsy because someone assigned it authority over the heart chakra. The Pet Rock was at least honest about its absurdity. It wore the joke on its sleeve, or rather, in its air holes. The crystal healing industry wears the joke on a silk pouch embroidered with a crescent moon and charges for the pouch.

The authors note that Dahl’s instruction manual — which included commands such as “sit” and “stay,” at which the rock consistently excelled — represented the highest obedience rate of any pet in recorded history. A 100% compliance rate. No feeding. No veterinary bills. No noise complaints. The ideal pet, the authors argue, was always a rock. The market simply wasn’t ready.

Peer Review Comments
I bought a Pet Rock in 1975. I have it on my desk. I am reviewing this paper next to it. I’m not sure what that says about me, but I suspect this paper is about to tell me. The rock, characteristically, has no opinion. Accept reluctantly.
The comparison to the crystal healing industry is devastating and I love it. Minor note: “pegmatite vein” should be “pegmatite body.” Also, rose quartz commonly forms in pneumatolytic environments, not just pegmatites. The error has been corrected in proof. The larger point — that we assign metaphysical properties to rocks based on color — stands unscathed. Revise and resubmit.
I am a rock. I have no opinion on this paper. I have no opinions. I lack the neural architecture for critique. I have existed for approximately 340 million years. Five stars. Accept (presumably).

Thermal Decomposition as Career Transition: A Longitudinal Study of Limestone-to-Cement Conversion and Its Implications for Geological Identity

Prof. Cal Cite1, Dr.ite ite2
1Department of Involuntary Processing, Portland Cement Research Institute  •  2Kiln Survivor Studies Program, University of Limestone
Received: ~340 Ma (as a marine organism)  |  Accepted: At 1,450°C  |  Published: As CO₂
Abstract
This paper traces the lifecycle of a single calcium carbonate unit from marine deposition (~340 Ma) through lithification, diagenesis, tectonic uplift, quarrying, and thermal decomposition at 1,450°C. We find that the cement production process destroys all geological identity in approximately 4 hours — a timeline 750 billion times shorter than the formation period. We ask whether this constitutes a career transition or an existential crisis. We do not answer, because the limestone can no longer be reached for comment.
Key Passage §3.1 — The Chemistry
The reaction is elegant in its brutality: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. Calcium carbonate, heated to 1,450°C in a rotary kiln, releases its carbon dioxide — the same carbon it has held in crystalline trust since the Carboniferous period — and becomes calcium oxide, or quickite. The CO₂ enters the atmosphere. The quicklite enters a mixer. The limestone’s 340-million-year identity is annulled in a thermal event that the industry describes, without irony, as “calcination.”
Key Passage §4.3 — The Scale
Global cement production: 4.1 billion tons annually. CO₂ released: approximately 2.8 billion tons, accounting for 5–8% of global anthropogenic emissions. Each ton of cement requires approximately 1.5 tons of limestone. The math is straightforward: to build the modern world, we are systematically disassembling the Carboniferous one. The marine organisms whose shells became this limestone fixed that carbon 340 million years ago. We are un-fixing it at industrial speed. The atmosphere is keeping score.

The authors conclude that “career transition” is an inadequate metaphor. A career transition implies the subject continues to exist in a new role. Limestone does not continue to exist after calcination. It becomes two things, neither of which is limestone. The appropriate metaphor, the authors suggest, is “disassembly.” But they note that “disassembly” is rarely used in industry literature, because it sounds like what it is.

Peer Review Comments
The title alone made me reconsider my career in cement research. I have spent 22 years studying clinker formation. I have never once framed it as “destroying a 340-million-year-old marine fossil record to make a sidewalk.” I am going to need a moment. Accept.
The “career transition” framing, while obviously satirical, raises genuine questions about how we narrate industrial processes. We say limestone “becomes” cement as if it’s voluntary. It is not. The passive voice in materials science papers is doing a lot of ethical heavy lifting. “Limestone is heated” — by whom? “CO₂ is released” — by what process, operated by what industry, for what purpose? This paper deserves publication and a therapy session. Accept with commendation.
Minor correction: “quickite” in §3.1 should read “quicklime.” The error has been preserved as a reminder that even fake journals have real typos.

Diamonds Are Forever, Graphite Is Now: A Comparative Study of Socioeconomic Outcomes for Allotropically Identical Carbon

Dr. C. Leavage1
1Department of Mineral Inequality Studies, De Beers Independent Research Center (citation needed)
Received: Under pressure  |  Accepted: Under more pressure  |  Published: Under even more pressure (this is also how diamonds form)
Abstract
Diamond and graphite are both pure carbon. Diamond is arranged in a tetrahedral lattice (sp³); graphite in planar sheets (sp²). Diamond retails for $5,000–$50,000 per carat. Graphite retails for $0.02 per pencil. This paper examines the socioeconomic consequences of crystallographic structure and concludes that geology has a class problem. We propose that no carbon atom should be valued more highly than another on the basis of bond angle alone, and we recognize that this proposal will be ignored.
Key Passage §2.1
The only difference between a $10,000 engagement ring and the core of a #2 pencil is the angle at which carbon atoms bonded under pressure. This is, quite literally, a structural inequality. The diamond had no more ambition than the graphite. It did not work harder. It did not attend a better university. It was subjected to different thermodynamic conditions in the upper mantle, emerged at the surface via kimberlite eruption, and was subsequently assigned a monetary value by a cartel. The graphite, equally carbon, equally ancient, writes standardized tests and is then discarded. The system is not meritocratic.
Key Passage §4.4
De Beers spent the 20th century convincing the public that a diamond — a compressed carbon lattice — was the only acceptable symbol of romantic commitment. They succeeded. An entire generation internalized the idea that love must be expressed in gigapascals. Meanwhile, graphite, the same element in a different mood, lubricates door hinges and fills in Scantron sheets. Both are carbon. Both are forever. One gets a vault; the other gets a pencil case. The market has spoken, and the market is crystallographically illiterate.

The paper closes with a meditation on lab-grown diamonds, which are chemically, physically, and optically identical to mined diamonds but cost approximately 70% less. If the consumer cannot tell the difference, the author argues, then the value was never in the carbon. It was in the story — specifically, the story that this carbon is rare, when in fact carbon is the fourth most abundant element in the universe. “You are not buying a rare element,” the author writes. “You are buying a marketing campaign that began in 1947.”

Peer Review Comments
“Structural inequality.” I see what you did there. I have been reviewing papers for 18 years and I have never encountered a pun that was simultaneously this groan-inducing and this scientifically precise. The sp³/sp² framing is correct. The class analysis is correct. The pun is unforgivable. All three are reasons to publish. Accept with applause.
The paper neglects to mention that lab-grown diamonds are chemically identical to mined diamonds and cost 70% less. If the market can’t tell the difference, the value was never in the carbon. It was in the story. This is either economics or geology. I’m no longer sure which department should evaluate this paper. I am forwarding it to both and to the philosophy department for good measure. Accept.

Towards a Unified Theory of Geological Neglect: Why Nobody Asks the Rocks

The Editorial Board
The Geological Review  •  Published in perpetuity
Received: 4.6 Ga  |  Accepted: After 4.6 billion years of deliberation  |  Published: Now, finally
Abstract
This paper asks a question that has not been asked in 4.6 billion years of geological study: why doesn’t anyone ask the rocks? We acknowledge immediately that rocks cannot answer, as they lack nervous systems, vocal cords, and opinions. But we note that this has never stopped humans from speaking on behalf of other entities — corporations, nations, unborn generations, fictional characters, and their pets. We propose that the omission is not scientific. It is cultural. And it is, if you think about it long enough, very funny. And then, if you think about it even longer, not funny at all. And then funny again. The cycle continues, much like the rock cycle, though faster.
Key Passage §2.1
Every geological process is described in the passive voice. Rock “is weathered.” Rock “is eroded.” Rock “is subducted.” The agent of these sentences — water, wind, gravity, humans — is grammatically secondary. The rock is always the object. Never the subject. This paper does not propose that rocks should be subjects. We propose that noticing this is the first step toward noticing other things we describe in the passive voice when we’d prefer not to name the agent. “Forests are cleared.” “Emissions are produced.” “Species are lost.” By whom? The passive voice knows. The passive voice isn’t telling.
Key Passage §3.4
We asked a piece of basalt for comment. The basalt did not respond. We asked again. Nothing. We waited for 45 minutes, which is approximately 0.000000000001% of the basalt’s lifespan. We interpreted the silence as neither consent nor refusal but as a reminder that some entities simply exist without narrating their existence. Humans find this unsettling. Rocks do not find it anything. That may be the point.

The paper closes with a simple observation: every atom in the human body was, at some point, part of a rock. The calcium in our bones was once limestone. The iron in our blood was once hematite. The silicon in our neural pathways was once quartz. We are, all of us, rocks that somehow learned to move and complain. The rocks we dig up and process and discard are our geological relatives. We are not suggesting you treat your driveway with familial respect. We are suggesting that the universe is stranger and more connected than the passive voice allows us to admit. And that noticing this — really noticing it — changes what you see when you look at a piece of gravel.

Peer Review Comments
This started as comedy and ended as philosophy. I was not prepared for the section on passive voice. I study erosion. I have written “rock is eroded” approximately 4,000 times in my career. I have never once written “water erodes rock” or “humans erode rock by redirecting water.” I am uncomfortable and impressed in equal measure. Accept.
The point about passive voice in scientific writing is… actually a real critique of how we narrate environmental destruction. I came here to laugh. I did not come here to think. I have been ambushed by a satirical geology journal. I need to sit down. I am already sitting down. I need to sit down harder. Reject on the grounds that I was not prepared.
Paper accepted unanimously. One reviewer submitted their review carved into a piece of slate. We respect the commitment. We were unable to scan the slate. We transcribed it by hand. The reviewer misspelled “sedimentary.” We corrected it. The slate did not object.
