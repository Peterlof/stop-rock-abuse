A Consumer Guide for the Rock-Conscious Individual

You're using rocks right now

You want to stop exploiting rocks. We respect that. So here is a comprehensive list of everyday products that contain, derive from, or were manufactured using rocks and minerals. Your task is straightforward: stop using all of them. We should mention upfront that this includes the device you're reading this on, the building you're sitting in, the roads that brought you here, and the electricity powering all of it. Good luck.

1. Construction & Home

Rock Guilt: Extreme

You literally live inside processed rocks.

Concrete & Cement
Limestone • Clay • Sand
Limestone, clay, and sand heated to 1,450°C. 4.4 billion tons produced annually. The single most consumed material on Earth after water.
You're probably inside some right now.
Bricks
Clay (weathered rock)
Clay—which is just rock that lost an argument with weather—shaped into blocks and fired in kilns. Humans have been baking rocks into building materials since 7500 BC.
Ten thousand years of kiln technology, all pointed at the same victim.
Glass / Windows
Silica Sand
Silica sand heated to 1,700°C until it becomes amorphous. The crystalline structure is permanently destroyed. What remains is transparent, which we find convenient.
Every window is a rock that lost its crystal structure.
Drywall / Plasterboard
Gypsum (Calcium Sulfate Dihydrate)
Gypsum mined, heated to drive off water, pressed into sheets, and installed as walls. The interior of your home is compressed mineral.
You hang pictures on flattened rock and call it interior design.
Roof Tiles & Slate
Slate (Metamorphic Rock)
Metamorphic rock that spent millions of years forming under heat and pressure, split into thin sheets and nailed to the top of your house to keep rain off.
Millions of years of geological formation. Current role: hat for your house.
Granite / Marble Countertops
Granite • Marble
Igneous and metamorphic rock polished to a mirror finish and installed in kitchens. A $40 billion global industry dedicated to giving rocks a second career as furniture.
Millions of years of geological formation, installed to hold your coffee mug.
Ceramic Tiles
Clay • Feldspar • Silica
Clay minerals mixed with feldspar and silica, shaped, glazed, and fired at high temperatures. Every tiled surface is kiln-tortured geology arranged in a grid.
Your bathroom floor is an organized display of mineral suffering.
Insulation (Mineral Wool)
Basalt • Slag
Rock melted at 1,500°C and spun into fibers at high speed, then stuffed inside walls. The rock equivalent of being turned into cotton candy and buried alive.
Melted, spun, and entombed. For your comfort.
Gravel / Crushed Stone
Various (Limestone, Granite, Trap Rock)
Rocks fed into industrial crushers and reduced to fragments. Used for driveways, foundations, and drainage. 1.3 billion tons per year in the US alone.
Water gets to evaporate and come back as rain. Rocks get crushed into gravel and poured under a highway.
Asphalt
Bitumen • Crushed Rock Aggregate
Bitumen mixed with crushed rock aggregate and spread in layers. The entire road network is built on this formula. Every road is a rock graveyard with lane markings.
We pave over rocks with other rocks, then drive on them.

2. Technology & Electronics

Rock Guilt: Severe

You're reading this on processed minerals right now.

Smartphones
Silicon • Lithium • Copper • Cobalt • Tantalum • Gold • REEs
Contains 60+ different minerals. Silicon for chips, lithium for the battery, copper for wiring, cobalt, tantalum, tin, tungsten, gold, and a full roster of rare earth elements.
Your phone is a geological greatest hits album that fits in your pocket.
Computers & Laptops
Silicon • Copper • Aluminum • Gold • Rare Earth Elements
The same mineral cocktail as a smartphone, in a larger format. The entire computing revolution runs on refined sand and smelted ore.
Silicon Valley is named after a rock element. They didn't try to hide it.
Fiber Optic Cables
Ultra-Pure Silica Glass
Silica purified to 99.9999% and drawn into hair-thin strands that carry data as light pulses. The backbone of the global internet is stretched, molten rock.
The internet runs on glass threads. The information superhighway is paved with sand.
Batteries (Lithium-Ion)
Lithium • Cobalt • Nickel • Manganese • Graphite
Lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese, and graphite, assembled into cells. Every EV battery contains roughly 8 kg of lithium and 35 kg of nickel.
Your EV battery is a mineral diversity initiative on wheels.
Solar Panels
Silicon • Aluminum • Silver • Copper
Silicon wafers, aluminum frames, silver contacts, copper wiring. Each panel requires about 20 grams of silver. "Clean energy" built entirely on mined minerals.
Harvesting sunlight with refined rocks. The sun does the work; the rocks get no credit.
LED Lights
Gallium • Indium • Sapphire
Gallium arsenide or gallium nitride on sapphire substrates. Your "energy-efficient" bulb exploits at least four minerals to produce photons.
Even the green option isn't rock-free. Nothing is.
Hard Drives
Aluminum • Neodymium • Platinum
Aluminum platters spinning at 7,200 RPM, read by heads floating on a cushion of air, positioned by rare earth magnets. Your vacation photos are stored on processed rocks.
Your data is scratched into spinning metal by a needle riding a magnet. Somehow this works.

3. Personal Care & Cosmetics

Rock Guilt: High

Your morning routine is a mineral extraction report.

Toothpaste
Calcium Carbonate • Silica • Fluorite
Contains calcium carbonate (chalk, which is limestone), silica (sand), and fluoride derived from the mineral fluorite. The abrasive that cleans your teeth is ground-up rock.
You scrub your teeth with powdered rocks twice a day and call it hygiene.
Deodorant
Aluminum Compounds (Bauxite Ore)
Many antiperspirants contain aluminum chlorohydrate, derived from bauxite ore. The mineral blocks your sweat glands. It does not get a say in this arrangement.
You apply processed rock to your armpits every morning. This is considered normal.
Makeup / Foundation
Talc • Mica • Iron Oxides • Titanium Dioxide • Zinc Oxide
Talc for smoothness, mica for shimmer, iron oxides for color, titanium dioxide for opacity, zinc oxide for coverage. A full mineral palette applied directly to the face.
Contouring is just strategic mineral placement.
Sunscreen
Zinc Oxide • Titanium Dioxide
Mineral sunscreens work by physically blocking UV radiation with a layer of zinc oxide or titanium dioxide particles. You smear mineral paste on your skin and call it protection.
A thin layer of rock between you and the sun. The rock takes the hit.
Exfoliating Scrubs
Pumice • Silica • Volcanic Rock
Pumice, silica, or ground volcanic rock suspended in gel. The product category where you use rocks to scrub dead skin cells—which formed on a body built with minerals—off your body.
You use rocks to remove parts of yourself. The circular economy at work.
Nail Polish
Mica • Silica • Iron Oxide
Mica for shimmer, silica for texture, iron oxide for color. The tiny minerals are suspended in solvent and painted onto keratin. Decorative geology at the fingertip level.
Glitter is just mica that got a rebrand.
Dry Shampoo
Kaolin Clay • Silica
Often contains kaolin clay or silica to absorb oil. You spray powdered geology into your hair to avoid washing it. The rocks do not judge you for this.
Dusting your hair with mineral powder is apparently easier than showering.

4. Kitchen & Food

Rock Guilt: High

Every meal involves mineral accomplices.

Salt
Halite (Sodium Chloride)
Halite. A mineral you eat deliberately, on purpose, multiple times a day. 300 million tons mined annually. The only rock with its own shaker on the dinner table.
You season your food with a mineral and nobody finds this remarkable.
Baking Soda
Nahcolite • Trona Ore
Sodium bicarbonate, derived from the mineral nahcolite or trona ore. It makes cakes rise, cleans surfaces, and deodorizes refrigerators. A mineral of many talents, none of them voluntary.
Ground-up rock is why your cookies are fluffy.
Cast Iron Pans
Iron Ore
Iron ore smelted at 1,200°C, poured into molds, and sold as cookware. People develop emotional attachments to these and pass them down as heirlooms.
Your omelet pan was a rock six months ago. Now it's a family heirloom.
Ceramic Dishes & Mugs
Kaolin Clay • Feldspar • Silica
Kaolin clay, feldspar, and silica shaped and fired. Your morning coffee arrives in a vessel made from three different minerals that were heated until they fused together permanently.
Your "World's Best Dad" mug is fired mineral. The text is iron oxide.
Aluminum Foil
Bauxite Ore
Bauxite ore refined through the Bayer process into alumina, then smelted and rolled into sheets 0.2mm thick. You use this to wrap leftover lasagna.
You wrap leftovers in processed rock and throw it away.
Stainless Steel Appliances
Iron Ore • Chromium • Nickel
Iron alloyed with chromium and nickel. Your refrigerator, dishwasher, and oven are alloys of at least three different rocks standing in your kitchen pretending to be appliances.
Your fridge is three minerals in a trench coat.
Water Filters
Activated Carbon • Zeolite • Diatomaceous Earth
Activated carbon, sometimes zeolite or diatomaceous earth. You pass water through rocks to remove things you don't want, which often includes other minerals.
You filter water through rocks to remove rocks from the water.
Non-Stick Coatings
Mineral-Derived Compounds
Some contain ceramic (mineral-based) compounds. Even the coating specifically designed so nothing sticks to it required minerals to manufacture. Rocks are inescapable.
Even the "nothing sticks" surface started as something that was stuck underground.

5. Transportation

Rock Guilt: Extreme

You can't go anywhere without riding on exploited rocks.

Roads
Asphalt • Crushed Stone Aggregate
Asphalt plus crushed stone aggregate, layered and compacted. The entire road network—4.19 million miles in the US alone—is a geological construction project masquerading as infrastructure.
Every road is a flat rock with painted lines and a speed limit.
Cars
Iron Ore • Bauxite • Silica • Mineral Pigments
Steel body (iron ore), aluminum panels (bauxite), glass windows (silica), rubber tires (mineral fillers), paint (mineral pigments). A car is a rolling mineral exhibition with cup holders.
You sit inside processed rocks traveling at 70 mph and complain about the traffic.
Trains & Rails
Steel • Crushed Stone Ballast
Steel rails bolted to wooden ties sitting on beds of crushed stone ballast. Trains literally ride on rocks, over rocks, using wheels made from rocks.
Trains ride on rocks. The rocks beneath them were placed there on purpose.
Aircraft
Aluminum Alloys • Titanium • Mineral Composites
Aluminum alloys for the fuselage, titanium for engine components, mineral-based composites throughout. Flying is one of the most mineral-intensive things a person can do.
You hurl refined rocks through the atmosphere at 500 mph and call it a vacation.
Tires
Silica • Calcium Carbonate • Carbon Black
Rubber compounded with silica, calcium carbonate, and carbon black. The part of the car that actually touches the road is part rock, touching more rock.
Rock-on-rock contact, 800 times per mile, and nobody files a report.
Brake Pads
Mineral Fibers • Copper • Iron • Ceramic
Mineral fibers, copper, iron, and ceramic compounds compressed into pads. Stopping your car works by grinding minerals against a metal disc until friction wins.
Stopping is just controlled rock destruction.

6. Clothing & Textiles

Rock Guilt: Moderate to Extreme

You are wearing rocks.

Zippers
Brass (Copper + Zinc) • Aluminum
Metal zippers are brass (copper and zinc ore) or aluminum. Plastic zippers often contain mineral-filled polymers. The mechanism holding your pants together is smelted rock.
Your fly is held up by refined ore. Act accordingly.
Buttons
Various Smelted Ores • Shell • Stone
Metal buttons are smelted ore. Some decorative buttons use actual stone or shell (calcium carbonate). The fastening industry is quietly mineral-dependent.
Buttons: small rocks pretending to be fashion accessories since the 13th century.
Dyes & Pigments
Iron Oxide • Cobalt • Chromium
Iron oxide for reds and yellows, cobalt for blue, chromium for green. Colored clothing is mineral-stained fabric. Your wardrobe is a pigment geology exhibit.
That shirt isn't blue. It's cobalt. The mineral just doesn't get a credit.
Synthetic Fabrics
Petroleum (Geological Origin)
Polyester is petroleum-derived, and petroleum is the product of geological processes acting on organic material over millions of years. Nylon follows the same lineage. Your athleisure has geological heritage.
Your yoga pants are ancient geology, technically.
Jewelry
Diamonds • Gold • Silver • Platinum • Gemstones
The most obvious entry on this list. Diamonds, gold, silver, platinum, gemstones—a $350 billion global industry built entirely on extracting rocks and attaching price tags.
The engagement ring industry is a rock marketing operation with excellent branding.

7. Medicine & Health

Rock Guilt: High

Your health depends on mineral exploitation.

Antacids
Calcium Carbonate (Limestone)
Calcium carbonate—limestone—compressed into tablets and flavored. You chew rocks to settle your stomach. Tums are literally flavored limestone.
Heartburn remedy: eat a rock. Medical science at its most direct.
Mineral Supplements
Calcium • Iron • Zinc • Magnesium • Potassium
Calcium, iron, zinc, magnesium, potassium—minerals extracted from geological sources, put in capsules, and sold at a significant markup. Supplements are rocks in pill form.
Rocks in capsule form, available at your pharmacy for $12.99.
Talcum Powder
Talc
Ground talc mineral, the softest mineral on the Mohs scale. Mined, pulverized, and dusted onto babies. Baby powder is powdered rock applied to the most vulnerable members of society.
We powder infants with ground-up rock and consider this standard childcare.
Kaolin (Anti-Diarrhea)
Kaolin Clay
A clay mineral used in some digestive medications. The treatment for certain stomach problems is to ingest geology. The rock absorbs the problem. Nobody thanks the rock.
Treating digestive issues with clay. The prescription is literally "eat dirt."
Medical Devices
Titanium • Ceramic • Surgical Steel
Titanium hip replacements, ceramic dental crowns, surgical steel instruments. Modern medicine would collapse without mineral exploitation. Your replacement knee is a rock.
Some people are part rock. They have the X-rays to prove it.
X-Ray Machines
Tungsten • Lead
Tungsten anodes generate the X-rays; lead shielding protects the operator. Seeing inside your body requires one mineral to emit radiation and another to block it.
Diagnosis by rock bombardment. The minerals don't get malpractice insurance.

8. Cleaning & Household

Rock Guilt: High

Cleaning your house requires destroying minerals.

Scouring Powder
Feldspar • Calcite • Silica
Products like Comet and Ajax contain feldspar, calcite, or silica. The cleaning strategy is straightforward: rub rocks on the stain until it leaves. The rocks wear down. The stain loses.
You clean your sink by scrubbing it with powdered rocks. This is the best method we have.
Cat Litter
Bentonite Clay • Diatomaceous Earth • Silica Gel
Bentonite clay (the most common type), diatomaceous earth, or silica gel crystals. Your cat's bathroom is a mineral pit. The cat does not appreciate the geological sacrifice involved.
Millions of years of mineral formation. Final assignment: cat bathroom.
Sandpaper
Aluminum Oxide • Silicon Carbide • Garnet
Aluminum oxide, silicon carbide, or garnet bonded to paper. A rock glued to paper, used to destroy other surfaces through abrasion. The concept is ancient. The execution is efficient.
A rock on paper, used to sand other things smooth. Weaponized geology.
Water Softener Salt
Halite • Potassium Chloride
Rock salt (halite) or potassium chloride pellets. You treat hard water—which is water that picked up too many minerals—by adding more minerals. The logic is circular but it works.
The cure for too many minerals is more minerals.
Porcelain Fixtures
Kaolin Clay
Toilets, sinks, and bathtubs: all fired kaolin clay. Every bathroom fixture is kiln-fired geology. Your bathroom is essentially a ceramics studio with plumbing.
You bathe inside a fired rock. You sit on a fired rock. Your bathroom is a kiln.
Steel Wool
Iron Ore
Very fine steel filaments made from iron ore. You scrub pots with shredded metal that was, not long ago, rock. The filaments slowly disintegrate from the friction, which is the point.
Shredded rock, used to scour other objects. Disposable geology.

9. Writing & Office

Rock Guilt: Moderate

Your office is a mineral depot.

Pencils
Graphite • Clay
Graphite mixed with clay, encased in wood. Writing with a pencil is the act of dragging a mineral across a surface until it leaves a trace. The pencil gets shorter. The writing remains.
Writing is mineral dragging. Every sentence costs a rock a fraction of its body.
Paper
Kaolin Clay • Calcium Carbonate
Contains kaolin clay coating for smoothness and calcium carbonate filler for opacity and brightness. Even paper—the thing you assumed was just trees—has rocks in it.
Paper is part rock. You've been writing on minerals this whole time.
Chalk
Calcium Carbonate
Pure calcium carbonate pressed into sticks. Teachers have been dragging rocks across blackboards for centuries. The chalk dust that covers everything is airborne mineral particulate.
Education runs on slowly disintegrating rocks. A metaphor writes itself.
Whiteboards
Porcelain • Steel
Porcelain-enameled steel. The replacement for chalkboards still uses minerals—just different ones. The upgrade from one rock surface to another was marketed as progress.
We replaced rock-on-rock with marker-on-rock and called it innovation.
Ink
Carbon Black • Iron Oxide • Titanium Dioxide
Contains mineral pigments: carbon black, iron oxide, titanium dioxide. The words on a printed page are mineral residue arranged into language.
Every printed word is a thin film of ground rock arranged to mean something.

10. Energy

Rock Guilt: Extreme

All energy involves rocks. Even renewables.

Coal
Coal (Sedimentary Rock)
Technically a sedimentary rock, burned for 27% of global energy. The most directly combusted rock. We dig it up and set it on fire. There is no subtlety to this process.
Step one: find rock. Step two: set rock on fire. We built civilization on this.
Natural Gas
Rock Formations (Shale, Sandstone)
Trapped in rock formations, extracted by drilling into those rocks or fracturing them (fracking). We break rocks open to get at the gas inside them. The rocks did not volunteer for this.
We crack rocks open to harvest their gas. This is a real industry.
Nuclear Power
Uranium Ore
Uranium ore mined, refined, enriched, and assembled into fuel rods. We split rock atoms to boil water to spin turbines. The most complicated way to make steam from rocks.
We split atoms from rocks to boil water. This is peak engineering.
Hydroelectric Dams
Concrete (Limestone + Rock Aggregate)
Massive concrete structures holding back rivers. The Hoover Dam contains 3.25 million cubic yards of concrete. We dam rivers with processed rock and call it renewable energy.
Renewable energy, built from non-renewable rocks. The fine print is geological.
Geothermal
Hot Rocks (Subsurface Geology)
We pump water into hot rocks deep underground and harvest the steam that comes back. The rocks do the heating. We do the billing. The rocks receive no royalties.
The rocks heat the water. We sell the electricity. The arrangement is one-sided.
Wind Turbines
Steel • Rare Earth Magnets • Concrete
Steel towers, rare earth magnets (neodymium, dysprosium) in generators, concrete foundations. Each turbine needs roughly 900 tons of steel and 2,500 tons of concrete. "Renewable" energy built on non-renewable minerals.
The wind is free. Everything used to capture it is mined.

11. Agriculture

Rock Guilt: High

Your food grows in pulverized rock.

Fertilizer
Phosphate Rock • Potash • Limestone
Phosphate rock for phosphorus, potash for potassium, limestone for pH correction. Industrial agriculture runs on ground-up rocks spread over fields. Crops grow because of mineral intervention.
Plants eat rocks so you can eat plants. The food chain starts underground.
Pesticides
Copper Sulfate • Mineral-Derived Compounds
Many contain mineral-derived compounds. Copper sulfate is a common fungicide. Sulfur is used as a pesticide. We spray mineral solutions on crops to protect them from other organisms.
Mineral-based pest control. The bugs don't stand a chance against geology.
Soil Itself
Weathered Rock + Organic Matter
Soil is weathered rock mixed with decomposed organic matter. The ground your food grows in IS rocks that have been broken down by weather, biology, and time over millennia. It takes about 500 years to produce one inch of topsoil.
Dirt is just rock that gave up. We grow lunch in it.

12. Entertainment & Sports

Rock Guilt: Moderate

Even fun involves rocks.

Golf Sand Traps
Silica Sand
Silica sand shaped into bunkers and maintained at significant expense. You hit a ball into a small pit of processed rock and get angry about it. The sand is indifferent to your handicap.
A sport where landing in rocks is a penalty. The rocks were there first.
Swimming Pools
Concrete • Ceramic Tile • Sand • Gypsum
Concrete shell (rock), tile finish (ceramic/rock), filtration sand (rock), plaster coat (gypsum). A swimming pool is a rock bowl filled with water. You pay to float in it.
Pools are rock bowls. You just add water and a pool noodle.
Ski Slopes
Crushed Rock Base • The Mountain Itself
Some slopes use crushed rock base layers. Artificial snow machines use mineral-rich water. And the mountain itself is, of course, an extremely large rock. The entire sport takes place on geology.
Skiing is sliding down a rock at speed. The snow is optional; the rock is structural.
Fireworks
Barium • Strontium • Copper • Aluminum
Barium for green, strontium for red, copper for blue, aluminum for white sparks. Fireworks are mineral explosions. You watch rocks detonate in the sky and feel patriotic about it.
Mineral explosions set to music. We do this to celebrate things.
Paint & Art Supplies
Cadmium • Cobalt • Ochre • Lapis Lazuli
Cadmium yellow, cobalt blue, ochre, ultramarine (historically from lapis lazuli). Art is the process of arranging colored rocks on a surface and calling it expression.
Every painting is just rocks smeared on fabric in a way that makes people feel things.
Musical Instruments
Bronze (Copper + Tin) • Brass (Copper + Zinc) • Steel
Cymbals are bronze (copper + tin), brass instruments are copper + zinc, drum hardware is steel. You strike, blow into, or vibrate smelted rock to produce sounds you find pleasing.
Music is organized vibrations of refined rocks. A cymbal crash is a rock screaming.

The uncomfortable conclusion is that modern human existence is entirely dependent on geological exploitation. Rocks are in your walls, your food, your medicine, your clothing, your transportation, your entertainment, and—if you have a titanium hip—literally inside your body.

Even this website is displayed on a device made from at least 60 different minerals, transmitted through fiber optic cables made from silica, powered by energy derived from geological sources, and read by eyes that require zinc, calcium, and iron to function.

We are not suggesting you stop using all of these products. That would require living naked in a field, which itself is weathered rock covered in organic matter. We are suggesting you feel slightly worse about it.
