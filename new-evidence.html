INCIDENT #NF-001 Freeze-Thaw Fracturing CASE OPEN — 3 BILLION YEARS
Date of Incident
Ongoing (every winter, every year, for approximately 3 billion years)
Location
Every rock face in every temperate climate on Earth
Suspect
Water (H₂O) — repeat offender
Victim
All exposed rock containing micro-fractures (i.e., all exposed rock)
Description of Incident

Water enters existing cracks in rock surfaces via capillary action and gravity. Upon freezing, water expands by approximately 9% in volume, exerting pressures up to 207 MPa (30,000 psi) on crack walls. This exceeds the tensile strength of most rock types (granite: ~7–25 MPa; sandstone: ~4–25 MPa; limestone: ~5–25 MPa). Cracks propagate. Rock fragments separate and fall. Process repeats with each freeze-thaw cycle — a typical alpine environment may experience 200+ cycles per year. The suspect is patient. The suspect is a molecule.

Officer’s Assessment

This is textbook mechanical weathering, specifically cryofracturing or frost wedging. The suspect — water — is exploiting a phase transition from liquid to solid to systematically dismantle rock structures from the inside. The method is patient, repetitive, and devastatingly effective. It has been operating without intervention for approximately 3 billion years. The suspect shows no signs of stopping, as it is governed by thermodynamics rather than criminal intent. We cannot arrest thermodynamics. We have tried.

Evidence Log

Exhibit A: Talus slopes at the base of every mountain range on Earth — piles of shattered rock, each fragment a victim of ice.

Exhibit B: Every pothole in Montreal. Every single one.

Exhibit C: The scree fields of the Scottish Highlands, where frost-shattered quartzite has been accumulating since the Younger Dryas (~12,900 years ago).

INCIDENT #NF-002 Chemical Dissolution CASE OPEN — ONGOING
Date of Incident
Continuous since the first rainfall on carbonate rock
Suspect
Carbonic Acid (H₂CO₃) — operating in conjunction with Rainfall
Victim
Limestone, marble, and any carbonate-bearing rock
Mechanism
CaCO₃ + H₂CO₃ → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻
Description of Incident

Rainwater absorbs CO₂ from the atmosphere, forming weak carbonic acid (pH ~5.6 for unpolluted rain). This acid reacts with calcite (CaCO₃) in limestone, dissolving it into calcium ions (Ca²⁺) and bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻). The reaction is slow by human standards — limestone surfaces typically erode at 1–10 mm per century — but over millions of years, this process creates cave systems, sinkholes, and karst topography that can undermine entire regions.

Officer’s Assessment

The suspect is literally dissolving the victim, ion by ion. The process is so slow that no single raindrop can be individually charged, creating a jurisdictional nightmare. Collective action by trillions of raindrops over millions of years has created the Mammoth Cave system in Kentucky — 420+ miles of surveyed passages — which is essentially a crime scene spanning 10 million years. The entire country of Slovenia is built on karst topography. Slovenia is, from a geological crime perspective, one big piece of evidence.

Evidence Log

Exhibit A: Carlsbad Caverns, New Mexico — a 120+ room cave system dissolved from Permian-age limestone.

Exhibit B: The entire country of Slovenia (20,270 km² of karst landscape, 14,000+ cataloged caves).

Exhibit C: The “Chocolate Hills” of Bohol, Philippines — 1,776 limestone mounds shaped by chemical weathering.

INCIDENT #NF-003 Glacial Abrasion CASE OPEN — INTERMITTENT
Suspect

Victim

Weapon
Embedded rock fragments (the geological equivalent of brass knuckles)
Speed of Approach
10–300 meters per year (slow, but the suspect does not need to hurry)
Description of Incident

Glaciers advance over bedrock, carrying embedded rock fragments — ranging from fine silt to house-sized boulders — frozen into their base. These fragments act as abrasives, scraping, gouging, and polishing the underlying bedrock as the glacier moves. The process is called glacial abrasion, and it operates with the efficiency of industrial sandpaper and the patience of something that measures time in ice ages. The glacier does not distinguish between rock types. The glacier does not care. The glacier is ice.

Officer’s Assessment

During the Last Glacial Maximum (~26,500 years ago), the Laurentide Ice Sheet covered North America to approximately 40°N latitude, reaching 4 km in thickness. Its weight depressed the crust by hundreds of meters (parts of Hudson Bay are still rebounding — a phenomenon called post-glacial isostatic adjustment). Every square meter of bedrock under that ice was scraped, grooved, or plucked. Striations visible in exposed bedrock today are the forensic evidence — parallel scratches scored into granite and gneiss by rocks dragged at the base of the ice. These are scratches. Measured in continents. The suspect has done this at least 5 times in the past 500,000 years alone.

Exhibit B: Glacial erratics — boulders transported hundreds of miles and abandoned when the ice melted. The “Big Rock” erratic near Okotoks, Alberta weighs 16,500 tons and was carried 50+ km from the Rockies.

Exhibit C: U-shaped valleys worldwide, from Yosemite to the Norwegian fjords — the calling card of glacial erosion.

INCIDENT #NF-004 Aeolian Erosion (Wind Abrasion) CASE OPEN — GLOBAL
Suspect
Wind — armed with sand particles traveling up to 100 km/h
Victim
Exposed rock in arid and semi-arid environments
Rate of Damage
Up to 1 mm of surface per year in active desert environments
Known Accomplices
Sand grains (quartz, Mohs 7 — harder than most victim rocks)
Description of Incident

Wind entrains sand-sized particles (0.0625–2 mm) through saltation — a bouncing transport mechanism where grains are lifted, arc through the air, and impact surfaces at high velocity. These impacts chip, pit, and polish exposed rock through a process called wind abrasion or aeolian corrasion. The result: ventifacts (wind-sculpted rocks with flat, polished faces called facets) and yardangs (streamlined bedrock ridges aligned with prevailing wind). In severe cases, wind erosion carves entire landscapes — the Lut Desert in Iran features yardangs up to 155 meters tall.

Officer’s Assessment

The suspect uses other rocks as ammunition against rocks. This is rock-on-rock violence facilitated by an invisible third party that cannot be detained because it is a pressure differential in the atmosphere. Ventifacts have been found in Antarctica — including on Mars (identified by the Curiosity rover) — proving the suspect operates in all planetary environments, even when no one is watching. Even when the nearest human is 140 million miles away. The suspect is thorough. The suspect is air.

Evidence Log

Exhibit A: Desert varnish — a thin, dark coating of manganese and iron oxides on rock surfaces, repeatedly sandblasted and recoated over millennia.

Exhibit B: Mushroom rocks (hoodoos) — top-heavy rock formations created because wind abrasion is most effective within 1 meter of the ground (the saltation zone).

Exhibit C: The Yardang National Geopark in Dunhuang, China — an entire city-sized landscape sculpted by wind.

INCIDENT #HO-002 Cement Production (The Kiln) CASE OPEN — 5-8% OF GLOBAL CO₂
Suspect
The Cement Industry (4,000+ plants worldwide)
Weapon
Rotary kilns operating at 1,450°C (2,640°F)
Annual Production
4.1 billion tons of cement per year
CO₂ per Ton
~0.6 tons (half chemical, half combustion)
Description of Incident

Limestone (CaCO₃) is quarried, crushed, and fed into rotary kilns — cylindrical furnaces up to 230 feet long, rotating at 1–3 RPM. At 900°C, calcination begins: CaCO₃ decomposes into CaO (quickite) and CO₂. At 1,450°C, the calcium oxide reacts with silica (SiO₂) and alumina (Al₂O₃) to form clinker — marble-sized nodules of calcium silicates. The clinker is cooled, mixed with gypsum, and ground into the fine gray powder called Portland cement. For each ton of cement produced, approximately 0.6 tons of CO₂ is released — roughly half from calcination (an unavoidable chemical reaction: you cannot heat limestone without releasing its carbon) and half from burning fossil fuels to reach 1,450°C.

Officer’s Assessment

The victim — limestone — spent millions of years forming on the seafloor from the calcareous shells and skeletons of marine organisms. Foraminifera, coccolithophores, corals: they lived, they died, their remains lithified into rock. That rock is now heated to a temperature that would destroy any evidence of its former inhabitants, then mixed with water and poured into the shape of a parking garage. The suspect calls this “construction.” The cement industry accounts for 5–8% of global anthropogenic CO₂ emissions — more than the entire aviation industry. If cement were a country, it would be the third-largest emitter on Earth. The parking garage does not know this.

Evidence Log

Exhibit A: China produced 2.1 billion tons of cement in 2022 — more than the rest of the world combined. In three years (2011–2013), China used more cement than the United States used in the entire 20th century.

Exhibit B: The chemical equation of calcination: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. This is not a choice. This is thermodynamics. The CO₂ was always in the limestone, placed there by organisms 300 million years ago. Humans just found a way to release it all at once.
