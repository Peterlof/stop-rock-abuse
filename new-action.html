EARTHCORP GEOLOGICAL EXPLOITATION

CLASS ACTION ENROLLMENT

Case No. 4,600,000,000-CV-0001  |  Filed: ~4.6 Ga  |  Status: Pending (the docket is backed up by several eons)

If you are a rock, or have ever been a rock, or contain minerals that were once part of a rock, you may be entitled to compensation.*

If you have consumed water that has eroded a rock, breathed air that has weathered a rock, or walked on ground that is, at every meaningful level, composed of rocks, you are also named in this action — as a defendant.

*Compensation is not available. Rocks cannot hold currency. This is a satire website. But the environmental recommendations below are real, and the geology is peer-reviewed by the kind of people who get excited about feldspars at parties.
Section I — For the Record

STATEMENT OF GRIEVANCES

The Defendant (Human Industry) has extracted approximately 50 billion tons of sand and gravel annually without consent from the Plaintiff class (all rocks). No environmental impact statement has been submitted to the rocks. No environmental impact statement can be submitted to the rocks. The Defendant has interpreted this silence as permission. The Plaintiff class interprets this silence as being a rock.
The Defendant has heated limestone to 1,450°C to produce 4.1 billion tons of cement annually, accounting for 5–8% of global CO₂ emissions. The chemical process — CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ — is irreversible at human timescales. The Defendant has described this as “making concrete.” The Plaintiff class describes this as “being incinerated and having your carbon expelled into the atmosphere to warm the planet that incinerated you.”
The Defendant has mined, refined, and discarded 30+ mineral species in the production of consumer electronics with a planned obsolescence of 24–36 months. A single smartphone contains silicon, aluminum, copper, lithium, cobalt, tantalum, tin, tungsten, and gold — materials that required between 10 million and 2 billion years to form. The Defendant considers these materials “outdated” when a new camera module becomes available.
The Defendant has created a recursive exploitation loop known as “concrete” — the process of destroying rocks to create inferior rocks, which themselves require rocks to maintain. Concrete cracks. The Defendant fills the cracks with more concrete. The cycle continues. The rocks have noticed. The rocks always notice. They just can’t say anything because they are rocks.
Section II — Proposed Resolution

TERMS OF SETTLEMENT

RECYCLED AGGREGATE USAGE

The Defendant agrees to utilize recycled aggregate in construction projects where technically feasible. Currently, only 5% of construction aggregate is recycled. The Plaintiff class requests this number increase to “literally any number higher than 5%, we’re not being unreasonable here.” The Plaintiff class notes that 5% is the kind of number you get when you aren’t trying. The Plaintiff class has seen your recycling bin. The Plaintiff class knows you aren’t trying.
Use and advocate for recycled aggregate in construction. Every ton of recycled aggregate is a ton of mountain that gets to remain a mountain.

CEMENT CARBON REDUCTION

The Defendant acknowledges that clinker substitution — replacing a portion of Portland cement clinker with supplementary cementitious materials such as fly ash, slag, or calcined clay — can reduce cement’s carbon footprint by 30–50%. The technology exists. The science is settled. The Defendant agrees to stop pretending this is complicated. It is not complicated. You heat rocks less. That is the entire proposal.
Support low-carbon cement alternatives. The cement industry knows how. It needs to know you’re watching.

ELECTRONIC DEVICE LONGEVITY

The Defendant agrees to stop manufacturing smartphones with 24-month obsolescence cycles. Every phone contains silicon, aluminum, copper, lithium, and tantalum — minerals that formed over millions of years. The minimum acceptable device lifespan should reflect at least 0.0000001% of the formation time of its components. This works out to approximately 10 years. The Defendant considers this unreasonable, which is itself unreasonable. The Defendant’s position is that a 50-million-year-old mineral becomes useless when the bezel gets thinner. The Plaintiff class finds this position geologically offensive.
Keep devices longer. Repair instead of replace. Your phone is a time capsule of geological history. Treat it like one.

QUARRY REHABILITATION

The Defendant agrees to rehabilitate exhausted quarry sites. A quarry is a hole where a mountain used to be. The Defendant does not get to walk away from a hole where a mountain used to be. The Plaintiff class notes that when the Defendant digs a hole in someone’s yard, the Defendant is expected to fill it. The Plaintiff class requests equivalent courtesy for mountains. The Defendant’s yard is smaller than a mountain. The Defendant’s yard is also made of rocks. The irony is not lost on the Plaintiff class.
Support quarry rehabilitation programs. A rehabilitated quarry can become a wetland, a nature reserve, or a lake. An abandoned quarry is just a scar with better drainage.

MINERAL TRANSPARENCY

The Defendant agrees to provide full supply chain transparency for all mineral extraction. Every rock in every product has an origin. The consumer has a right to know which mountain their phone case used to be part of. The Plaintiff class notes that the Defendant labels the country of origin on a banana but not on a piece of lithium that was extracted from a salt flat at 3,800 meters elevation in the Atacama Desert. The banana took 9 months to grow. The lithium took several million years. The banana gets the label.
Support mineral supply chain transparency. Know where your materials come from. The rocks can’t tell you. Somebody should.
Section III — Constitutional Amendment (Proposed)

THE ROCK BILL OF RIGHTS

We hold these truths to be self-evident — or they would be, if rocks had a concept of self, or evidence, or truth. They do not. They are rocks. But we proceed regardless, because someone has to, and the rocks certainly aren’t going to.

IArticle
The right to remain unheated above ambient geological temperatures.
Currently violated 4.1 billion times annually by the cement industry. “Ambient geological temperature” for surface limestone is approximately 15°C. The cement kiln runs at 1,450°C. The difference is 1,435°C, or as the industry calls it, “standard operating procedure.”
IIArticle
The right to complete a full rock cycle transformation without industrial interruption.
Average rock cycle duration: 200 million years. Average quarry intervention: whenever a highway needs repaving. A sedimentary rock may spend 80 million years becoming a metamorphic rock. It does not appreciate being turned into a driveway at year 40 million.
IIIArticle
The right to be correctly identified.
Calling all dark rocks “basalt” is profiling. Calling all shiny rocks “quartz” is lazy. There are over 5,000 known mineral species. Learn at least five. Granite, basalt, quartz, feldspar, mica. There. You’ve started. That was not difficult. The rocks are not impressed, but they weren’t going to be anyway.
IVArticle
The right to not be described as “just a rock.”
You are also “just a collection of carbon-based molecules animated by electrochemical impulses,” but we don’t call you that at parties. A piece of granite is a record of magmatic cooling, crystallization, tectonic uplift, and erosion. It has a longer and more interesting biography than most LinkedIn profiles.
VArticle
The right to exist without being turned into a countertop, a headstone, or a piece of costume jewelry.
Rocks predate you by billions of years. They are not your decorative accessories. They are geological elders. You would not sand down a redwood to make a coaster. Actually, you would. You absolutely would. The Plaintiff class rests its case.

Section V — Sidebar from the Author

WHAT YOU CAN ACTUALLY DO

OK. Real talk. The satire is fun, but the environmental issues are actual. Cement production really does account for 5–8% of global CO₂. Sand mining really is unsustainable at current rates. E-waste really is a crisis. Here are things actual humans can actually do:

USE RECYCLED AGGREGATE

Recycled concrete aggregate can replace virgin materials in many construction applications. Ask about it. Specify it. It’s not exotic — it’s just crushed old concrete.

UNEP Sand Sustainability Report →

SUPPORT CEMENT DECARBONIZATION

Low-carbon cement technologies exist today. Clinker substitution, carbon capture, and alternative binders can cut emissions by 30–50%. The industry needs market demand and regulatory pressure.

IEA Cement Industry Roadmap →

EXTEND DEVICE LIFESPANS

Keep your phone for at least 4–5 years. Repair it when it breaks. Every year you extend a device’s life reduces its annualized environmental impact by a meaningful margin.

iFixit — Repair Guides →

SUPPORT RESPONSIBLE MINING

Mining isn’t going away, but it can be done better. Support companies that commit to environmental remediation, fair labor, and supply chain transparency.

LEARN WHERE MATERIALS COME FROM

Every object around you has a geological origin story. Understanding the supply chain is the first step toward improving it. The USGS tracks mineral commodities globally.
